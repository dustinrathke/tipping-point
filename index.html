Cheap power now, or a livable island later

Every turn you build — towns, farms, coal, wind, solar, forests. Coal is the cheapest, strongest choice on the board, and for ten turns nothing goes wrong. Then farms wither, forests burn, and the sea starts taking your lowest ground, tile by tile.

Warming lags behind carbon. By the time you feel it, more is already on the way. That isn't a message on a loading screen — it's the rule that decides whether you win.

The mechanics are the lesson

In the gameIn the world
Temperature keeps climbing after you stop emittingCommitted warming
The ocean absorbs less carbon as it warmsWeakening carbon sinks

Boilerplate: Tipping Point is a turn-based strategy game about growth, energy, and consequences that arrive late. Players grow an island settlement to 1,500 people while a small, honest climate simulation runs underneath: warming lags emissions, the ocean sink weakens, wildfires feed back, and the sea takes the low ground first. Coal is always the cheapest choice — that's the point. The game teaches tipping points without ever saying "climate change."
