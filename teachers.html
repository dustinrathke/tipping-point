TIPPING POINTClassroom guide — a strategy game that teaches climate tipping points without ever saying "climate change"

Grades: 6–12 Time: 30–45 min Cost: free Devices: any browser — Chromebook, iPad, phone Accounts: none · no student data collected Grouping: solo or pairs
▶ Play: https://dustinrathke.github.io/tipping-point/play/ — start with Island mode; Archipelago adds storms, seawalls, and adaptation trade-offs for older students.

What students do

Students govern a small island: build towns, farms, and power to grow from 120 to 1,500 islanders. Coal is the cheapest and strongest power source every turn — but a climate simulation runs underneath, and its consequences arrive on a delay. Most students' first island collapses. That collapse is the lesson.

Where the science shows up

In the gameThe science
Temperature keeps rising for turns after emissions stopCommitted warming / climate lag
The ocean absorbs less CO₂ as it warmsWeakening carbon sinks
Wildfires destroy forests and release their carbon, causing more warmingPositive feedback loops
The sea floods the lowest tiles first, at temperature thresholdsSea-level rise; tipping points
Coal is the best per-turn deal on the board, yet coal-heavy islands end up poorerDelayed & externalized costs
Background "world" emissions the player can't controlCollective action — local sinks still matter

Suggested lesson (~40 min)

  1. 5 min — Setup. Share the link. Give only one instruction: "Grow your island to 1,500 people." No other guidance.
  2. 10 min — Run 1. Most students build coal and boom for ten turns. Many islands collapse. Let them.
  3. 10 min — Discuss. Use questions 1–3 below. Lag, sinks, and feedbacks will surface from their own runs.
  4. 10 min — Run 2. New challenge: win while staying under +1.6°. Compare strategies afterward.
  5. 5 min — Exit ticket. "What would you tell your turn-1 self?"

Discussion questions

  1. When did your island start being in trouble — and when did you first notice? What explains the gap?
  2. The ocean quietly absorbed carbon for you early on. Why did it stop? What else started feeding the warming?
  3. Coal was the cheapest choice every single turn. Why did players who kept choosing it end up poorer?
  4. You couldn't control the rest of the world's emissions. Did your choices still matter? What changed your outcome?
  5. The game never uses the words "climate change." What is it about?

Standards alignment

  • NGSS MS-ESS3-5 — factors causing the rise in global temperatures: students identify causes directly from their own emissions-vs-sinks readout each turn.
  • NGSS HS-ESS3-6 — use a computational representation of relationships among Earth systems and human activity: the game is one, and students manipulate it directly.
  • AP Environmental Science, Unit 9: Global Change — greenhouse gases, global climate change, ocean warming, positive feedback loops (topics 9.3–9.6).
